The rise of quantum computing


In the 1980’s, Richard Feynman and Yuri Manin introduced the concept of quantum computing, a method for computing which is 100x better than normal computing. They used quantum mechanics and quantum physics and quantum everything else to create quantum computing. Since then, this field of technology has evolved significantly, especially in the 2010’s and 2020’s decade. The question is, what is quantum computing and how has it evolved? Before we dive deep, let’s define some simple terms:


Quantum

The tiniest of units of any kind of physical property, normally talking about particles such as electrons, photons, etc.

Qubit

A special kind of bit (classic bits are either 0 or 1, basically binary) that can be both 0 and 1


Quantum computing

The application of quantum physics to computers and programming.


Quantum machine learning

Using the power of quantum mechanics, we can use it to train better models, aka better AI’s.

Quantum Search

A search function that is much faster than your average Google at finding your desired result from your search. It is also known as Grover’s Algorithm, developed in 1996, and is mostly used in databases or for very advanced math related things.


Quantum interference

Where particles’ possible states have the ability to combine together or conflict with each other, affecting the particle’s outcome.


Quantum Simulation

Modeling other kinds of quantum scenarios. A normal computer models normal scenarios, which is the same process for quantum computers to be able to model quantum scenarios.

Cryptography

Turning valuable information into unreadable characters as part of securely keeping them safe.


Optimization

Determining the best solution for something while considering its benefits and limitations (EX: pros and cons of investing in solar panels)


Entanglement

an ability of quantum particles to be connected with each other. If we know the state of one quantum particle, we automatically know the state of its connected particle(s) as well, even if they’re not near each other. Basically, quantum soulmates.

Superposition

A qubit has a superposition, meaning it can be both 0 and 1 at once.





Traditional computing is super cool and common these days. However, they cannot handle advanced computing and methods like quantum computing can. There is an increasing need for advanced computing anyway due to more knowledge of the world also increasing, needing detail to be explained accurately. That’s when quantum computing comes in, a wonderful idea developed that uses existing knowledge of quantum mechanics/physics to progress these computation systems. However, as of right now, such advanced machinery is currently experimental, meaning that they are not released out to the public like basic desktops and video game consoles are. Quantum computers are used in databases for conducting special scientific research. Due to their out-of-this-world performance, they are to be used wisely since they are only the best solution to the most complicated of problems. They are big-brain computers used for big-brain purposes, while our commonly used computers are fit for our basic needs.
