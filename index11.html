ChatGPT is bad for the environment, here’s why



Many people have started to use AI almost daily for even the tiniest tasks ever. One popular form goes by the name of ChatGPT, powered by OpenAI. Commonly used through its website interface and its app, people have grown dependent on ChatGPT. Even schools and universities are tired of students misusing that app. Not only that, but people use it for other purposes such as therapy sessions or asking the dumbest questions on Earth that would get them laughed at if asked to another human. However, the increased usage of ChatGPT has led to environmental concerns, particularly through its water usage.



Before we dive into the issues with ChatGPT, let me tell you the process used by this entity to grant you a wonderful response. Basically, your sent input goes into a data server located in an AI center where the AI can mimic, detect questions to answer, compare to existing internet sources, then respond to your input using its generated text with help of that mimicry, detection, and comparison, which helps predict every word. Now, diving into the main issue with water consumption, those data servers are what’s using so much water. To prevent overheating, they are submerged in water 24/7 to keep functioning under decent temperatures. Water does evaporate when heated up, though, so more water needs to replace the amount of water lost to keep the data servers cool. That means more water is being used for this purpose. Now, one or two usages of ChatGPT may not seem like a big deal. However, with the millions that are using this technology, the sum of water used to cool down the hardworking data servers is quite big. According to the Washington Post’s research collaboration with the University of California, one ChatGPT prompt requesting a 100-word email uses about 519 millimeters of water, which is equal to around one 16-ounce Poland Spring water bottle. This seems insignificant right now, but if we multiply it by the millions who use ChatGPT for more than one prompt daily, it results to be very significant.



What could this mean for the environment? It could mean very much. That significant amount of water could at least have been used for a better purpose. Going back to the introduction, the many other purposes millions of people use ChatGPT for are included but not limited to therapy sessions and dumb questions. Who knows how many more useless purposes there could be? Sure, they could be fulfilled here and there with ChatGPT’s help. However, millions are OVERUSING it, meaning more water goes to fulfill their purposes instead of possibly saving a town from a massive drought. One way to help decrease water usage is to start using your own brain and resources around you. Do you need to express your struggles often? Vent to a family member, a friend, or to a therapist! Do you need much more help than just a one-time assistance with your school subjects? Hire a tutor or go to a tutoring center! Do you have a burning question needing to be answered? Research manually or use Deepseek (a better-for-the-environment AI)! That can help level up your comprehension skills too. You can also be saving GALLONS of water this way while getting the help you need in better form, and those saved gallons could prevent many droughts or water shortages, which can also be helpful for the environment.



While ChatGPT is a promising and convenient way of receiving the knowledge or help you need within seconds, it is NOT a sustainable service. Limiting AI usage for day-to-day tasks is crucial for keeping the environment intact and preventing further harm. Using other forms of AI such as Deepseek, which is a much better choice for the environment yet offers better help on logic-based subjects.
