A Beginner's Guide to Investing



Investing, the art of producing long term income from buying different kinds of assets, mostly stocks, bonds, or real estate, is a very good way to make money, oftentimes more in quantity and faster in speed than leaving money in a savings account. However, that money is not guaranteed, since it can also easily be lost based on your investing skills and the companies you invest in. Starting to invest can also be plentifully challenging. To combat that, here is a foolproof method to start investing the proper way.

Step 1: Identify and write down what your goals are and what you are investing for.

Diving into the stock market can be 100x easier if you go in with a definite purpose. Whether investing in stocks could be for retirement or college, knowing what you’re risking money (yes, risking money) for will help you be more serious in decision making. Oftentimes, when people invest without a purpose, they do not care as much about what they may be losing (too much flexibility in risk tolerance), therefore being very loose and reckless. That is NOT supposed to happen. Money is a tool to take seriously, and it is much easier to take seriously when you know what the money is aimed for. It can prevent about 75% bad decisions.

Step 2: Figure out what investing account best fits your goals

Here are the common kinds of accounts for investing

Brokerage account

Using this account is decent for making investments not specific to retirement or college. Usually, bonds/funds/stocks/ETFs are bought through the account. However, you must be 18+ to open it and you will get taxed yearly on your investments.

Traditional/Roth IRA

The IRA is focused on retirement. It is definitely useful if you don’t have a 401(k)/403(b) option and is more flexible than those options. Roth IRAs are for beginners, while traditional IRAs fit more for intermediates in investing due to low-high income differences.


401(k)/403(b)

These are employer sponsored accounts that you automatically get opened for you at a job. Both accounts are investments for retirement. However, 401(k)’s are for private organization workers (EX: corporate) while 403(b)’s are for public workers (EX: teachers).


Step 3: Open your chosen account

Now that you have the account you figured would fit best for your investment needs, it is time to open the account either at work (401(k)/403(b)) or at a financial institution (brokerage/IRA). When at the step of putting money into your account, you mustn’t over-invest. Investing a small amount of money at first is definitely better than investing a big amount and possibly losing it. That money possibly could have paid your electricity bill! KEY RULE: pay all your bills/necessities, then invest the rest.

Step 4: Invest wisely

Diversification, the act of buying stocks from many different companies rather than just a few, will significantly decrease the risk of you losing your money. Do not invest in just one company. There will come a time when the stock market will not favor some companies, and it is typically the ones who invested tons into limited amounts of companies who lose the most amount of money. However, if you have trouble with investing in stocks, you can refer to a professional manager who will manage your investing for you, even though this service is pretty expensive.




Now that you have a wonderful outline provided to you on how to start investing, you are prepared to head into the stock market. Now that you also have an idea of what stocks you plan to buy, buy them. Buy a couple of shares from a couple of companies and just watch your money grow! (if you did it right)
